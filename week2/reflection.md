# Week 2 Reflection

The main shift in my Week 2 strategy was moving from a relatively broad adaptive search to a much more disciplined trust-region approach. After reviewing the Week 1 results, it was clear that some functions were responding well to local refinement, while others were not rewarding the direction I had taken. With only a limited number of rounds left, I decided the priority should be sample efficiency rather than coverage. In practical terms, that meant exploiting tightly where Week 1 improved performance, refining cautiously where the result was plausible but not better, and resetting to the best historical region where the Week 1 move underperformed.

I also added explicit sanity checks before finalising the submission. Rather than relying on model output alone, I checked whether each proposed point stayed close to the best known basin, whether it remained within the intended trust region, how strong the nearest observed outputs were, and whether any variable had been pushed to an extreme boundary value without enough evidence. That was probably the most important operational improvement this week. It made the process feel less like trusting the model and more like using the model as one input into a structured decision.

That sanity-check process directly changed one of the final selections. For Function 5, the original candidate was in a strong-performing area, but it drifted outside the local trust region and pushed one feature all the way to the upper boundary. From a management perspective, that looked like an unnecessarily aggressive use of a scarce query. I replaced it with a more conservative local exploitation point that preserved the same high-performing pattern but stayed closer to the best region. I left the other functions unchanged because, even where they were not perfect, they were still consistent with the Week 2 logic.

I used different techniques for different groups of functions. For the lower-dimensional functions, especially Functions 1 to 4, I leaned more on local GP-style reasoning and geometric checks around the best observed point. At this dimensionality, it is still possible to reason about basins and local movement in a fairly intuitive way. For the higher-dimensional functions, Functions 5 to 8, I used local surrogate-guided search, mainly random-forest-style reasoning, but only within a constrained neighbourhood rather than allowing broad global jumps. I also grouped the functions behaviourally: Functions 5, 6, and 8 were treated as momentum cases because Week 1 produced clear gains; Functions 2 and 4 were cautious-refinement cases; and Functions 1, 3, and 7 were recovery cases where I felt the right move was to return to the best historical region and search nearby.

So this week was more exploitative than exploratory. That was a deliberate choice. The trade-off was straightforward: I could spend guesses learning more about uncertain regions, or I could spend them trying to improve regions that had already shown evidence of performance. Given the remaining budget, I chose the latter unless the previous direction had clearly failed.

If I step back and look at this as someone about ten weeks into an ML course, the key lesson is that modelling alone is not enough. The real value came from combining model suggestions, empirical outcomes, and disciplined judgement. That feels very similar to executive decision-making in general: use the analytics, but put guardrails around them, especially when the cost of a bad move is high and the number of opportunities is limited.
